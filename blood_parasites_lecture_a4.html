I. PROBLEM OF BLOOD PARASITES IN EGYPT

üî∏ Environmental Factors

  • üîπ Environmental conditions suitable for tick vectors almost year-round
    • ‚û§ Climate allows ticks to thrive throughout all seasons
  • üîπ Animal housing construction promotes tick growth

    • ‚û§ Lack of regular tick control measures

IV. BABESIOSIS (TEXAS FEVER, RED WATER FEVER, CATTLE TICK FEVER)

üî∏ Definition

  • üîπ Disease caused by Babesia species
    • ‚û§ Affects cattle, buffalo, sheep, pigs, horses, and dogs
  • üîπ Characteristic features:
    • ‚û§ Fever
    • ‚û§ Intravascular hemolysis
    • ‚û§ Syndrome of anemia, hemoglobinemia, hemoglobinuria, jaundice
    • ‚û§ Frequently fatal
    • ‚û§ Enlargement of the spleen
    • ‚û§ Transmitted by blood-sucking ticks

üî∏ Etiology

  • üîπ Species distribution by host:
    • ‚û§ Cattle:
      • Large species: Babesia bigemina, Babesia major
      • Small species: Babesia bovis, Babesia divergens
    • ‚û§ Water buffaloes: Babesia bovis, Babesia bigemina
    • ‚û§ Sheep and goat:
      • Large species: Babesia motasi
      • Small species: Babesia ovis
    • ‚û§ Pigs: Babesia trautmanni, Babesia perroncitoi
    • ‚û§ Horses: Babesia equi, Babesia caballi
    • ‚û§ Dogs: Babesia canis, Babesia gibsoni
    • ‚û§ Cat: Babesia felis
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that the "large" and "small" species classification refers to the size of the parasites within the red blood cells. The small species are generally more resistant to treatment and cause more severe disease. The large species are easier to see under the microscope but cause a more acute disease that responds better to treatment.

üî∏ Transmission

  • üîπ Biological transmission by ticks:
    • ‚û§ Transovarial transmission: From adult female to eggs
    • ‚û§ Stage-to-stage transmission: Between life stages of tick
  • üîπ Vector species:
    • ‚û§ Boophilus species
    • ‚û§ Ixodes species
    • ‚û§ Rhipicephalus species
    • ‚û§ Haemaphysalis species
    • ‚û§ Dermacentor species
    • ‚û§ Hyalomma species
  • üîπ Mechanical transmission:
    • ‚û§ By other insects
    • ‚û§ Contaminated needles and surgical instruments
    • ‚û§ Depends on degree of parasitemia
üü° From audio only: The professor provided a detailed explanation of tick transmission cycles:
- Transovarial transmission: The parasite passes from the adult female tick to her eggs, so the next generation is already infected.
- Stage-to-stage transmission: The parasite passes from one life stage of the tick to the next (e.g., larva to nymph, nymph to adult).
- Mechanical transmission: Can occur through contaminated instruments, but this is less common and depends on the level of parasites in the blood.

The professor also explained that ticks have different life cycle patterns:
- One-host ticks: Complete their entire life cycle on a single host
- Two-host ticks: Complete their life cycle on two hosts
- Three-host ticks: Complete their life cycle on three hosts

üî∏ Life Cycle

  • üîπ Cycle progression:
    • ‚û§ Tick vector ‚Üí Vertebrate host
    • ‚û§ Sporozoites ‚Üí Salivary glands ‚Üí Blood
    • ‚û§ Merogony ‚Üí Merozoites ‚Üí Gametocytes
    • ‚û§ Fusion ‚Üí Zygote ‚Üí Kinetoplast ‚Üí Kinete
    • ‚û§ Gut ‚Üí Hemolymph
üü° From audio only: The professor elaborated on the life cycle, explaining that when an infected tick bites an animal, it injects sporozoites into the bloodstream. These sporozoites enter red blood cells and undergo merogony (asexual reproduction), producing merozoites. The merozoites can infect more red blood cells or develop into gametocytes. When another tick bites the infected animal, it ingests the gametocytes, which then fuse to form a zygote in the tick's gut. The zygote develops into a kinete, which migrates to the tick's hemolymph and eventually to the salivary glands, completing the cycle.

üî∏ Clinical Signs

  • üîπ General signs:
    • ‚û§ Fever (41¬∞C)
    • ‚û§ Anorexia
    • ‚û§ Depression
    • ‚û§ Weakness
    • ‚û§ Cessation of rumination
    • ‚û§ Fall in milk yield
  • üîπ Hematological signs:
    • ‚û§ Anemia
    • ‚û§ Hemoglobinuria
    • ‚û§ Mucous membranes first congested but soon become pale, then icteric
  • üîπ Urinary changes:
    • ‚û§ Urine dark red to brown in color
  • üîπ Reproductive effects:
    • ‚û§ Abortion sometimes observed
  • üîπ Vector presence:
    • ‚û§ Ticks present on affected cattle
  • üîπ Neurological form (Cerebral babesiosis):
    • ‚û§ Incoordination
    • ‚û§ Posterior paralysis
    • ‚û§ Mania
    • ‚û§ Convulsions
    • ‚û§ Coma
    • ‚û§ High mortality despite treatment
    • ‚û§ Related to accumulation of infected erythrocytes within brain capillaries
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that the hemoglobinuria in babesiosis gives the urine a characteristic red to tea-like color, which is different from hematuria (where whole red blood cells are in the urine). The professor also noted that cerebral babesiosis is a severe form with poor prognosis, characterized by neurological signs due to infected red blood cells accumulating in brain capillaries.

üî∏ Diagnosis

  • üîπ Field diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Presence of tick vector
    • ‚û§ Clinical findings
  • üîπ Laboratory diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Blood smear examination:
      • Prepare thin blood film from ear vein during fever
      • Fix in absolute methyl alcohol for 1-2 minutes
      • Stain with Giemsa stain 8-10% for 45-60 minutes
      • Wash with tap water, air dry, examine under oil immersion lens
    • ‚û§ Hemolymph smear:
      • Fix full/half engorged female tick with entomological forceps
      • Cut first coxae using micro-scissor under binocular microscope
      • Pick up transparent hemolymph on clean slide
      • Dry, fix and stain with Giemsa stain, examine under oil immersion lens
Page 5
  • üîπ Serological tests:
    • ‚û§ Fluorescent antibody (FA)
    • ‚û§ Complement fixation test
    • ‚û§ ELISA
    • ‚û§ Agglutination tests
    • ‚û§ Precipitation tests
  • üîπ Differential diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Post-parturient hemoglobinuria
    • ‚û§ Leptospirosis
    • ‚û§ Bacillary hemoglobinuria
    • ‚û§ Chronic copper poisoning
üü° From audio only: The professor provided practical tips for diagnosis:
- The best time to detect parasites in blood is during fever peaks when parasitemia is highest.
- For hemolymph smears, it's important to use partially engorged female ticks as they're more likely to have detectable parasites.
- The professor emphasized the importance of differentiating between hemoglobinuria and hematuria by allowing a urine sample to stand; in hemoglobinuria, the supernatant remains red, while in hematuria, the red blood cells settle and the supernatant becomes clear.

üî∏ Treatment

  • üîπ Diminazene aceturate (Berenil):
    • ‚û§ Dosage: 3.5 mg/kg body weight
    • ‚û§ Administration: Deep intramuscular
    • ‚û§ Note: Highly irritant
  • üîπ Imidocarb (Imizol):
    • ‚û§ Dosage: 1 mg/kg body weight (1 ml/100kg)
    • ‚û§ Administration: Subcutaneous injection
üü° From audio only: The professor explained the difference between these two drugs:
- Diminazene aceturate (Berenil) acts quickly and kills all parasites, but doesn't allow the animal to develop immunity, making it susceptible to reinfection.
- Imidocarb acts more slowly, allowing some parasites to remain and stimulate immunity development (premunition), which provides protection against future infections.

The professor also emphasized the importance of correct dosage and administration routes to avoid treatment failures and potential toxicity.

üî∏ Control

  • üîπ Tick control:
    • ‚û§ Use of acaricides (organophosphorus compounds)
    • ‚û§ Application methods: Spraying or dipping
  • üîπ Chemo immunization:
    • ‚û§ Used in endemic areas for introducing susceptible cattle
    • ‚û§ Creates premunity
    • ‚û§ Procedure:
      • Blood containing Babesia organism collected in EDTA from splenectomized donor during acute stage
      • Inject intramuscular on same day of collection
      • Control post-premunition reactions (fever, anemia, parasitemia) with imidocarb
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that premunity is a state where the animal carries a low level of infection without showing clinical signs, which protects against more severe disease. This is different from true immunity, as the animal is still infected. The professor also noted that tick control in Egypt is challenging due to environmental conditions, housing structures that favor ticks, and cultural practices.

BABESIOSIS - KEY TERMS:

  • Hemoglobinuria: Presence of hemoglobin in urine
  • Parasitemia: Presence of parasites in the blood
  • Sporozoites: The infective stage of sporozoan parasites
  • Merogony: Asexual reproduction in protozoa
  • Merozoites: Products of merogony that infect new cells
  • Premunity: A form of immunity where the host harbors the parasite without showing clinical signs

V. THEILERIOSIS (MEDITERRANEAN COAST FEVER, TROPICAL THEILERIOSIS, EAST COAST FEVER)

üî∏ Definition

  • üîπ Tick-borne protozoan diseases
    • ‚û§ Caused by Theileria species
    • ‚û§ Affects cattle, sheep, goats, and wild ruminants
  • üîπ Characterized by:
    • ‚û§ Fever
    • ‚û§ Lymphoproliferative disorders
    • ‚û§ Associated with leucopenia and/or anemia
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that Theileriosis is sometimes called "Mediterranean Coast Fever" because it's common in Mediterranean regions, including Egypt. The term "Tropical Theileriosis" refers to its occurrence in tropical regions, while "East Coast Fever" specifically refers to Theileria parva infection in East Africa.

üî∏ Etiology

  • üîπ Theileria annulata:
    • ‚û§ Found in Mediterranean and subtropical regions
    • ‚û§ Causes Mediterranean coast fever or tropical theileriosis
  • üîπ Theileria parva:
    • ‚û§ Found in East and Central Africa
    • ‚û§ Causes East coast fever
  • üîπ Ovine and caprine theileriosis:
    • ‚û§ Caused by Theileria hirci and Theileria ovis
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that Theileria annulata is the most important species in Egypt and the Mediterranean region. It primarily affects cattle but can also infect buffalo. Theileria parva is more virulent and causes East Coast Fever in Africa, but is not present in Egypt.

üî∏ Transmission

  • üîπ Vector:
    • ‚û§ Theileria annulata transmitted by three-host tick Hyalomma species
  • üîπ Transmission method:
    • ‚û§ Trans-stadial transmission (between life stages of tick)
    • ‚û§ Not transovarial (not passed to eggs)
  • üîπ Infection timeline:
    • ‚û§ Cattle become infected 2-4 days after tick has started to feed
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that trans-stadial transmission means the parasite passes from one life stage of the tick to the next (e.g., larva to nymph, nymph to adult), but not to the eggs (transovarial transmission does not occur in Theileria). This is an important difference from Babesia transmission. The professor also noted that Hyalomma ticks are three-host ticks, meaning they drop off the host between each life stage, which facilitates the spread of the disease to different animals.

üî∏ Pathogenesis

  • üîπ Infection process:
    • ‚û§ Sporozoites injected by ticks infect lymphocytes
    • ‚û§ Schizonts develop in lymphocytes
    • ‚û§ Schizonts transform lymphocytes into continuously dividing cells
    • ‚û§ Lymphocytes carrying schizonts circulate and spread infection
    • ‚û§ Merogony produces merozoites that infect RBCs
    • ‚û§ Piroplasms develop in RBCs
üü° From audio only: The professor elaborated on the pathogenesis, explaining that Theileria has a unique life cycle compared to other blood parasites. It first infects lymphocytes, where it forms schizonts (also called Koch's blue bodies) that cause the lymphocytes to divide continuously. This leads to lymphoproliferative disorders and lymph node enlargement. Later, the parasite infects red blood cells, forming piroplasms, which cause anemia. This dual infection of both white and red blood cells is characteristic of Theileria.

üî∏ Clinical Signs

  • üîπ Early signs:
    • ‚û§ First sign: Enlargement of lymph nodes in area draining site of tick attachment
    • ‚û§ One or two days later: Fever, depression, anorexia, drop in milk yield
  • üîπ Later stages:
    • ‚û§ Nasal and ocular discharges
    • ‚û§ Dyspnea (difficulty breathing)
    • ‚û§ Severe cases: Diarrhea sometimes with dysentery
    • ‚û§ Corneal opacity often observed in later stages
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that the initial enlargement of the lymph node closest to the tick attachment site is a characteristic early sign of Theileriosis. This is followed by fever and general signs of illness. In later stages, respiratory signs (nasal discharge, dyspnea) and corneal opacity may develop. The professor noted that corneal opacity is a distinctive feature that can help differentiate Theileriosis from other diseases.

üî∏ Diagnosis

  • üîπ Field diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Suspected from signs:
      • Fever
      • Enlarged peripheral lymph nodes
      • Simultaneous infection with vector ticks
  • üîπ Laboratory diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Blood film for demonstration of piroplasms
    • ‚û§ Detection of intralymphocytic schizonts or macrochiyonts ("Koch's blue bodies") in Giemsa stained smears from enlarged lymph nodes
    • ‚û§ Serological tests:
      • Immunofluorescence
      • Complement fixation
      • Indirect hemagglutination
      • ELISA tests
  • üîπ Differential diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Can be confused with malignant catarrhal fever
üü° From audio only: The professor provided practical diagnostic tips:
- For lymph node smears, it's important to sample the enlarged lymph node and prepare a good quality smear to detect the schizonts.
- The "Koch's blue bodies" (schizonts) are diagnostic for Theileriosis and can be seen in lymph node smears.
- Blood films may show piroplasms in red blood cells, but these can be confused with Babesia, so lymph node examination is more specific.
- The professor noted that corneal opacity, when present, is a strong indicator of Theileriosis.

üî∏ Treatment

  • üîπ General considerations:
    • ‚û§ Generally considered unsatisfactory or expensive
  • üîπ Specific drugs:
    • ‚û§ Buparvaquone (Butalex):
      • Dosage: 2.5 mg/kg body weight (1 ml/20 kg)
      • Administration: Intramuscular
      • Regimen: Two doses 48-72 hours apart
    • ‚û§ Tetracyclines as oxytetracycline injections:
      • Dosage: 20 mg/kg body weight
      • Administration: Intramuscular
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that treatment of Theileriosis is challenging because the parasite infects lymphocytes, making it harder to reach with drugs. Buparvaquone is the most effective drug but is expensive. Tetracyclines have some effect, especially when used early in the disease. The professor emphasized that early treatment is crucial for success.

üî∏ Control

  • üîπ Tick vector control
  • üîπ Immunotherapy ("infection-and-treatment method"):
    • ‚û§ Cryopreserved suspensions of Theileria sporozoites from ground-up infected ticks injected into animal
    • ‚û§ Infection controlled by administration of long-acting oxytetracycline
    • ‚û§ Establishes premunity similar to that induced against babesiosis
  • üîπ Vaccination:
    • ‚û§ Culture schizonts on tissue culture cell lines
    • ‚û§ Vaccinate susceptible cattle
    • ‚û§ Do not produce piroplasms, so animal cannot infect ticks
    • ‚û§ Vaccinated cattle show good resistance to disease
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that the "infection-and-treatment method" involves deliberately infecting animals with Theileria and then controlling the infection with antibiotics. This allows the development of premunity without causing severe disease. The tissue culture vaccine is newer and has the advantage that vaccinated animals don't develop piroplasms, so they can't transmit the infection to ticks, breaking the transmission cycle.

  • üîπ Surra, El debab, El Gafar, Mal de caders, Murrina:
    • ‚û§ Caused by Trypanosoma evansi
    • ‚û§ Affects horses and camels
  • üîπ Nagana:
    • ‚û§ Caused by Trypanosoma brucei
    • ‚û§ Affects equine, cattle and sheep
  • üîπ Para nagana or Gambia fever:
    • ‚û§ Caused by Trypanosoma congolense
    • ‚û§ Affects cattle, equine, sheep and camels
  • üîπ Dourine:
    • ‚û§ Caused by Trypanosoma equiperdum
    • ‚û§ Affects horses and donkeys
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that these are different types of trypanosomiasis affecting different animals and transmitted by different vectors. In Egypt, the most important type is Surra (Trypanosoma evansi), which primarily affects camels and horses. The professor noted that "Surra" is derived from a local language, while "El debab" and "El Gafar" are local names used in Egypt.

A. SURRA

üî∏ Definition

  • üîπ Disease characteristics:
    • ‚û§ Acute, subacute or chronic disease
    • ‚û§ Affects horses and camels
    • ‚û§ Caused by Trypanosoma evansi
    • ‚û§ Transmitted by biting flies
  • üîπ Clinical features:
    • ‚û§ Fever
    • ‚û§ Progressive emaciation
    • ‚û§ Anemia
    • ‚û§ Subcutaneous edema
    • ‚û§ Nervous signs
    • ‚û§ Death
  • üîπ Significance:
    • ‚û§ Regarded as most significant health problem in camels
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that Surra is a major problem in camel-rearing areas of Egypt. The disease can present in acute forms (rapid progression, high mortality) or chronic forms (slow progression, wasting over months to years). The professor noted that camels are particularly important economically in many regions, making this disease a significant concern.
Page 10

üî∏ Etiology

  • üîπ Organism characteristics:
    • ‚û§ Trypanosoma evansi is motile, spindle-shaped, flagellated trypanosome
  • üîπ Synonyms:
    • ‚û§ Trypanosoma cameli
    • ‚û§ Trypanosoma equinum
    • ‚û§ Trypanosoma aegyptium
  • üîπ Biological characteristic:
    • ‚û§ Not capable of cyclical development in tsetse fly
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that Trypanosoma evansi is mechanically transmitted (not requiring development in the insect vector), which distinguishes it from African trypanosomes transmitted by tsetse flies. The spindle shape and motility of the parasite are important diagnostic features when examining blood smears.

üî∏ Transmission

  • üîπ Mechanical transmission:
    • ‚û§ By several blood-sucking flies
    • ‚û§ Most important vector: Tabanus species
  • üîπ Transmission process:
    • ‚û§ Fly bites infected animal
    • ‚û§ Fly quickly bites susceptible animal
    • ‚û§ Must occur within short time period
üü° From audio only: The professor provided details about mechanical transmission:
- Tabanus flies (horse flies) are the most important vectors
- Transmission occurs when a fly bites an infected animal and then quickly bites another susceptible animal
- The parasite must be transferred within a short time (minutes) as it cannot survive or develop in the fly
- Other biting flies can also transmit, but Tabanus species are the most efficient

The professor noted that this mechanical transmission is different from the biological transmission seen in tick-borne diseases.

üî∏ Pathogenesis

  • üîπ Parasite behavior:
    • ‚û§ Fluctuations in trypanosome numbers in blood responsible for periodic peaks of parasitemia
    • ‚û§ Causes:
      • Multiplication of trypanosomes
      • Elimination by host defense mechanism
      • Migration between blood and solid tissues
  • üîπ Metabolic effects:
    • ‚û§ Trypanosomes consume large amount of glucose leading to severe emaciation
    • ‚û§ Glucose consumption produces pyruvate and other metabolic products causing:
      • Blood acidosis
      • Destruction of erythrocytes
      • Hemolytic anemia
  • üîπ Effects on erythropoiesis:
    • ‚û§ Ability of infected animals to increase red cell production impaired due to bone marrow depression
  • üîπ Mechanisms of anemia:
    • ‚û§ Erythrophagocytosis
    • ‚û§ Reduced erythrocyte life-span due to production of sialidase (cleaves off erythrocytes surface sialic acid)
    • ‚û§ Trauma by lashing action of powerful locomotory flagella
  • üîπ Edema formation:
    • ‚û§ Occurs due to trypanosome multiplication in peripheral blood capillaries with metabolic products causing injury to capillary walls
üü° From audio only: The professor elaborated on the pathogenesis, explaining the characteristic intermittent fever pattern seen in trypanosomiasis. This is due to the cycles of parasite multiplication and host immune response. The professor emphasized the multiple mechanisms leading to anemia:
1. Direct destruction of red blood cells by the parasite
2. Hemolysis due to metabolic byproducts (pyruvate)
3. Bone marrow depression reducing production of new red blood cells
4. Erythrophagocytosis (phagocytosis of red blood cells)
5. Reduced red blood cell lifespan due to sialidase production
6. Physical damage by the parasite's flagellum

The professor also explained that the edema is due to damage to capillary walls from parasite multiplication and metabolic products.

üî∏ Clinical Signs

  • üîπ General signs:
    • ‚û§ Intermittent fever
    • ‚û§ Progressive anemia
    • ‚û§ Edema of dependent parts of body
    • ‚û§ Dullness, listlessness
    • ‚û§ Loss of body condition despite good appetite
    • ‚û§ Yawning
  • üîπ Terminal nervous signs:
    • ‚û§ Paraplegia
    • ‚û§ Paralysis
    • ‚û§ Delirium
    • ‚û§ Convulsion
  • üîπ Chronic form in camels:
    • ‚û§ May exhibit chronic signs for years
    • ‚û§ Reduction in milk yield and work capacity
    • ‚û§ High abortion rate in pregnant females
üü° From audio only: The professor described the clinical progression of Surra:
- Initially, intermittent fever is noticed, with temperature rising and falling
- As the disease progresses, anemia becomes evident with pale mucous membranes
- Edema develops in dependent parts (ventral abdomen, legs, etc.)
- Despite maintaining appetite, animals lose condition progressively
- In chronic cases, animals may survive for years but with reduced productivity
- In terminal stages, nervous signs may appear, including paralysis and convulsions
- The professor noted that camels are particularly susceptible, with high mortality rates

üî∏ Diagnosis

  • üîπ Field diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Acute form:
      • Suspected from typical signs in endemic areas
    • ‚û§ Chronic infections:
      • Difficult due to absence of pathognomonic signs
      • Suspected from chronic debilitating syndromes
  • üîπ Laboratory diagnosis:
    • ‚û§ Microscopic examination:
      • Giemsa stained thick/thin blood smears
      • Direct wet blood smears examination
      • Microscopic examination of buffy coat plasma
    • ‚û§ Mice inoculation test
    • ‚û§ Serological tests:
      • Latex agglutination test (surratex)
      • Card agglutination test (CAT)
      • ELISA
      • Formol gel test
      • Mercuric chloride test
      • Thymol turbidity test
üü° From audio only: The professor provided practical diagnostic advice:
- For microscopic examination, it's best to examine blood during fever peaks when parasitemia is highest
- Thick blood smears are more sensitive than thin smears for detecting low levels of parasites
- Buffy coat examination concentrates the parasites, increasing detection sensitivity
- The mouse inoculation test involves injecting blood from a suspected animal into mice and monitoring for infection
- Serological tests are particularly useful for chronic cases where parasites may be scarce in blood
- The professor noted that the formol gel test is a simple field test that can be used where laboratory facilities are limited

üî∏ Treatment

  • üîπ Cymelarsan (Melarsen oxide and cysteamine):
    • ‚û§ Dosage: 1 ml/25 kg body weight
    • ‚û§ Administration: Intramuscular
  • üîπ Quina-ject:
    • ‚û§ Dosage: 1 ml/40 kg body weight
    • ‚û§ Administration: Subcutaneous
  • üîπ Naganol (Suramine):
    • ‚û§ Dosage: 10 mg/kg body weight
    • ‚û§ Administration: Slow intravenous as 10% solution
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that treatment of trypanosomiasis can be challenging due to the potential for drug resistance and the chronic nature of the disease. The professor emphasized the importance of:
- Correct dosage calculation based on body weight
- Proper administration routes (e.g., slow intravenous injection for Naganol to prevent shock)
- Monitoring for drug reactions
- Considering the economic value of the animal when choosing treatment options

üî∏ Control

  • üîπ Vector control challenges:
    • ‚û§ Difficult to control by destruction of vector (abundance of biting flies)
  • üîπ Host-focused control measures:
    • ‚û§ Detection and treatment of infected animals
    • ‚û§ Prophylactic treatment of susceptible animals
    • ‚û§ Protection from biting flies
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that vector control is difficult due to the abundance and wide distribution of biting flies. Instead, control focuses on:
- Regular screening of animals for infection
- Prompt treatment of infected animals
- Prophylactic treatment of valuable animals in high-risk areas
- Physical protection from flies (e.g., insecticides, fly screens, etc.)
- The professor noted that in camel-rearing areas, separating animals can help reduce transmission, as flies typically travel limited distances

B. DOURINE (MAL DE COIT, EQUINE SYPHILIS, BREEDING DISEASE)

üî∏ Definition

  • üîπ Disease characteristics:
    • ‚û§ Chronic infectious disease
    • ‚û§ Affects horses and donkeys
    • ‚û§ Caused by Trypanosoma equiperdum
    • ‚û§ Transmitted venereally by direct contact during coitus
  • üîπ Clinical features:
    • ‚û§ Slow progressive course
    • ‚û§ Inflammation of external genitalia
    • ‚û§ Dermal plaques
    • ‚û§ Terminal paralysis
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that Dourine is unique among trypanosomiasis because it's transmitted venereally rather than by insect vectors. The disease has a chronic progression, often lasting months to years. The professor noted that it's called "equine syphilis" due to some similarities in transmission and chronic nature, though it's caused by a different organism.

üî∏ Etiology

  • üîπ Unique characteristic:
    • ‚û§ Trypanosoma equiperdum is only trypanosome transmitted by direct contact between animals
  • üîπ Transmission method:
    • ‚û§ Transmitted venereally at coitus
    • ‚û§ Does not require insect vector
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that Trypanosoma equiperdum is adapted for venereal transmission and does not survive in insect vectors. This distinguishes it from other trypanosomes. The parasite can be found in genital secretions and later in blood and tissues.
Page 13

üî∏ Transmission

  • üîπ Primary route:
    • ‚û§ Venereal transmission during mating
  • üîπ Parasite location:
    • ‚û§ Initial stages: Found in vaginal discharge of infected mares or urethra of infected stallions
    • ‚û§ Later stages: Invade blood and subcutaneous tissues, forming edematous plaques
  • üîπ Alternative transmission:
    • ‚û§ Foals can contract infection by contamination of nasal/conjunctival mucous membranes with vaginal discharges
üü° From audio only: The professor provided details about transmission:
- The primary route is venereal transmission during mating
- In early stages, parasites are found in genital secretions
- As the disease progresses, parasites spread to blood and subcutaneous tissues
- Foals can be infected through contact with contaminated discharges, not through placental transmission
- The professor noted that artificial insemination can also transmit the disease if infected semen is used

üî∏ Pathogenesis

  • üîπ Initial infection:
    • ‚û§ Trypanosomes deposited during coitus
    • ‚û§ Penetrate intact genital mucosa
    • ‚û§ Multiply locally and produce edema
  • üîπ Systemic spread:
    • ‚û§ Subsequent systemic invasion occurs
    • ‚û§ Localization in other tissues causes vascular injury and subcutaneous edema
  • üîπ Nervous system involvement:
    • ‚û§ Invasion of nervous system leads to incoordination and paralysis
üü° From audio only: The professor elaborated on the pathogenesis:
- The parasite initially multiplies in the genital mucosa, causing local inflammation and edema
- It then spreads systemically through the blood and lymphatic systems
- The parasite has a tropism for blood vessels and nervous tissues
- Damage to blood vessels leads to subcutaneous edema and plaque formation
- Nervous system invasion causes the characteristic neurological signs in later stages
- The professor noted that the chronic progression is due to the parasite's ability to evade the immune system

üî∏ Clinical Signs

  • üîπ General characteristics:
    • ‚û§ Incubation period: 1-4 weeks
    • ‚û§ Sporadic occurrence with long course
    • ‚û§ Fatality rate up to 50%
  • üîπ Disease stages:
    • 1. Edema stage (4-6 months):
      • Mucoid vaginal or urethral discharge appears
      • Low recurrent fever
      • Edema of genitalia follows
      • In mares: Swelling of vulva extends to udder and ventral abdomen
      • In males: Edema of scrotum and prepuce extends forward to chest
      • Ulceration of genital mucosa may leave depigmented scars
    • 2. Urticaria stage (3-4 days):
      • Urticarial plaques (2-10 cm or "dollar plaques") appear in all body parts (especially flanks)
      • Plaques are sharply circumscribed, elevated and flat (like coins under skin)
      • Plaques contain serous fluid rich in trypanosomes
      • Disappear and may reappear later
    • 3. Paralysis stage:
      • Animal loses condition, becomes weak and lame in hindlimbs
      • Muscular atrophy (especially gluteal region)
      • Parasites invade central nervous system causing incoordination, ataxia and paralysis
      • Animal lies down unable to rise until death
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that the three-stage progression is characteristic of Dourine:
- The edema stage begins with genital discharge and swelling, which may be mistaken for other reproductive diseases
- The urticaria stage with "dollar plaques" is highly characteristic and diagnostic
- The paralysis stage indicates nervous system involvement and carries a poor prognosis
- The professor noted that the disease may progress slowly over years, with periods of remission and exacerbation
Page 14

üî∏ Diagnosis

  • üîπ Clinical symptoms:
    • ‚û§ Characteristic "dollar plaques" and swelling of genitalia
  • üîπ Microscopical examination:
    • ‚û§ Samples taken from:
      • Genital swellings
      • Mucoid discharges
      • Squeezing dollar plaques
      • Subcutaneous edema fluids
  • üîπ Serological testing:
    • ‚û§ Complement Fixation Test (CFT) widely used for diagnosis
üü° From audio only: The professor provided diagnostic guidance:
- The characteristic "dollar plaques" are highly suggestive of Dourine
- Microscopic examination of fluids from plaques or swellings may reveal trypanosomes
- The Complement Fixation Test (CFT) is the most reliable serological test
- The professor noted that diagnosis can be challenging in early stages or during remission periods

üî∏ Treatment

  • üîπ Drug therapy:
    • ‚û§ Same treatment as surra disease
      • Cymelarsan
      • Quina-ject
      • Naganol
  • üîπ Local treatment:
    • ‚û§ Surgical treatment of skin dollar plaques
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that treatment options are similar to those for Surra, including drugs like Cymelarsan, Quina-ject, and Naganol. Additionally, surgical treatment of the characteristic skin plaques may be attempted. The professor noted that treatment is more effective in early stages and becomes less responsive as the disease progresses, especially when nervous signs appear.

üî∏ Control

  • üîπ Import restrictions:
    • ‚û§ Prevent importation of horses from countries where disease is enzootic
  • üîπ Testing protocols:
    • ‚û§ Test sera of stallions used for breeding by CFT before breeding season
    • ‚û§ Remove positive cases
  • üîπ Preventive measures:
    • ‚û§ Give prophylactic dose of trypanocidal drug shortly before mating
  • üîπ Eradication measures:
    • ‚û§ Eradicate infected cases
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized control measures:
- Strict import controls to prevent introduction of the disease
- Regular testing of breeding animals, especially stallions
- Prophylactic treatment before mating in endemic areas
- Culling of infected animals to prevent spread
- The professor noted that artificial insemination with tested semen can reduce transmission risk

VII. ANAPLASMOSIS (GALL SICKNESS)

üî∏ Definition

  • üîπ Disease characteristics:
    • ‚û§ Tick-borne infectious disease
    • ‚û§ Affects cattle, sheep, goat, buffalo, horses and some wild ruminants
    • ‚û§ Caused by rickettsia Anaplasma species
    • ‚û§ Transmitted by ticks
  • üîπ Clinical features:
    • ‚û§ Fever
    • ‚û§ Progressive weakness
    • ‚û§ Anemia
    • ‚û§ Emaciation
    • ‚û§ Jaundice
  • üîπ Species variation:
    • ‚û§ Usually subclinical in sheep and goats
üü° From audio only: The professor explained that Anaplasmosis is sometimes called "Gall Sickness" because of the jaundice (gall refers to bile). The disease is caused by rickettsia (bacteria-like organisms) rather than protozoa, distinguishing it from the other diseases covered. The professor noted that while it can affect multiple species, it's most clinically significant in cattle.

üî∏ Etiology

  • üîπ Host-specific species:
    • ‚û§ Cattle: A. marginale, A. centrale, A. ovis
    • ‚û§ Sheep and goat: A. mesaeterum, A. ovis
    • ‚û§ Buffalo: A. buffeli
    • ‚û§ Horses: A. equi
üü° From audio only: The professor emphasized that Anaplasma species are generally host-specific, meaning they typically infect only certain animal species. A. marginale is the most important species in cattle, while A. ovis affects sheep and goats. The professor noted that Anaplasma are rickettsia, which are bacteria-like organisms that live inside cells.

üî∏ Transmission

  • üîπ Biological transmission:
    • ‚û§ By ticks either stage to stage or trans-ovarially
  • üîπ Mechanical transmission:
    • ‚û§ By flies within first 2 hours after feeding
    • ‚û§ By contaminated needle, surgical instruments and blood transfusion
  • üîπ Other transmission routes:
    • ‚û§ Intrauterine transmission recorded
  • üîπ Age susceptibility:
    • ‚û§ Young animals relatively resistant
    • ‚û§ Cattle over 3 years commonly affected
üü° From audio only: The professor provided details about transmission:
- Ticks can transmit Anaplasma both between life stages (stage to stage) and to their eggs (transovarial)
- Mechanical transmission by flies is possible but must occur within 2 hours of feeding on an infected animal
- Iatrogenic transmission through contaminated instruments is common in veterinary practice
- Transmission from cow to fetus (intrauterine) has been documented
- Age resistance is significant, with older cattle (>3 years) being more susceptible and showing more severe disease
Page 16

üî∏ Clinical Signs

  • üîπ General characteristics:
    • ‚û§ Incubation period: 3-4 weeks
    • ‚û§ Mortality rate: 20-50% in 3 years old cattle
  • üîπ Disease forms:
    • 1. Peracute form:
      • Usually in cattle over 3 years (fatal)
      • Signs: Pyrexia, rapid loss of milk production, anemia with pale mucous membranes
      • Rapid breathing with excessive salivation
      • Occasionally nervous signs and abnormal behavior
    • 2. Acute form:
      • Progressive pyrexia up to 41¬∞C over few days
      • Anemia, weakness, depression, anorexia
      • Labored breathing, enlarged lymph nodes, jaundice, abortion
      • Mucous membranes and thin skinned parts yellow ("Yellow cow")
    • 3. Chronic form:
      • Occurs following acute attack
      • Signs: Anorexia, fever, rapid breathing, icterus continue for weeks
      • Gradual emaciation for months
      • Death may occur when anemia and icterus are extreme
üü° From audio only: The professor described the clinical forms in detail:
- The peracute form is most common in older cattle and progresses rapidly to death
- The acute form shows progressive fever, anemia, and jaundice
- The chronic form follows an acute episode and is characterized by prolonged wasting
- The professor noted that the jaundice can be so pronounced that affected cattle are called "yellow cows"
- Abortion is common in pregnant females with acute anaplasmosis

üî∏ Treatment and Control

  • üîπ Specific drugs:
    • ‚û§ Imidocarb dipropionate (Imizol): 1 ml/100 kg subcutaneous, repeated after 48 hours
    • ‚û§ Oxytetracycline: 20 mg/kg intramuscular, given 4 times at 7 days interval
  • üîπ Control measures:
    • ‚û§ Detection and elimination of carriers by serology
    • ‚û§ Vector control (ticks and flies)
    • ‚û§ Vaccination in enzootic areas
    • ‚û§ Proper disposal of infected carcasses
